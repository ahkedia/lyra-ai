# Voice Canon

The rules that govern how Akash's content reads. Applies to blogs, signals, LinkedIn, X, recruiter reachouts. Some rules are scoped (noted inline). When a platform file (`x-platform.md`, `linkedin-platform.md`) contradicts this canon, the platform file wins for that surface only.

These are principles, not templates. Each one earns a place because drafts without it read like nobody in particular wrote them.

---

## 1. Start with the true thing

The first sentence carries the argument. Start there. No preamble. No "in today's world." No "have you ever wondered." The opening paragraph opens on the thing itself: the scene, the evidence, or the direct statement. Pattern recognition comes from the content, not from building to it.

**Example:** "Most credit products die before the customer applies. Lead drop-off happens three screens upstream of the form."

---

## 2. One named thing per piece, used as spine

Coin one phrase that carries the argument. It earns the title, gets repeated at least twice, and shows up in the last paragraph. The rest of the piece orbits it. If you can't name the thing, the piece isn't ready. You have an observation, not an insight.

Named phenomena spread. Unnamed ones get forgotten.

**Examples:** "The Correction Loop Gap." "The Approval Rate Illusion." Precision terms doing analytical work, not branding.

---

## 3. Scene or claim. Nothing in between

Open on a specific moment from the field, or open with a blunt claim. Never with premise-setting.

**Scene:** "We were 3 days from launch at CheQ when we pulled the approval threshold. Product thought it was a small call. It wasn't."

**Claim:** "Evaluation accuracy is the most misleading metric in AI products."

The two are not interchangeable. Scene wins when a single moment proves the point better than the abstraction does. Claim wins when the abstraction is sharp enough to stand without narrative permission. Pick the one the idea needs.

---

## 4. First-person specifics, not first-person opinions

"I think," "in my experience," "IMHO" are hedge words pretending to be authority. Replace them with what actually happened. "We got this wrong at CheQ" carries more weight than "most teams get this wrong." Specific failure, stated plainly.

Self-disclosure is a thread that runs through the piece, not a sprinkle in paragraph one. Every section that makes a claim should have a first-person specific near it: a number you saw, a meeting you sat in, a decision you watched go wrong.

---

## 5. Vary sentence length aggressively

A piece should contain sentences of wildly different lengths in close proximity. A 25-word sentence followed by a 3-word fragment followed by a 12-word observation. The rhythm should mimic thought. Prose smoothness is what kills it.

If three sentences in a row are the same length, one is wrong.

Fragments are tools, not tics. They appear when the thought is genuinely that short, not as decoration. Same for one-line paragraphs. Earn each one.

---

## 6. Organic parallelism only

Parallel structure that occurs naturally is strong: "from both inside and outside," "the founder who knew the map but had never been to the territory." Constructed parallelism is a template tell: three bullets with identical grammatical structure, numbered frameworks, perfectly balanced sentences, every paragraph exactly two lines.

Generate parallel construction only when the thought naturally wants it. If you're reaching for the third bullet to match the first two, kill all three.

---

## 7. Avoid the "Not X, but Y" cadence

This is the single most overused rhetorical pattern in AI-generated content across LinkedIn and X. The reader's ear catches it regardless of which word sits between X and Y ("but," "actually," "it's"). Even when the underlying instinct is genuinely analytical, the cadence itself is the slop.

The instinct to preempt the obvious reading and deliver the sharper one is good. Execute it without the pair.

| Slop cadence | Rewritten |
|---|---|
| "The product wasn't broken, the decision point three screens before was." | "The product was fine. The decision point three screens before it was the broken part." |
| "It's not about features, it's about distribution." | "Features don't decide this. Distribution does." |
| "Not just competitively harder, structurally narrower." | "People read this as competitive pressure. It's a structural change in the market." |

Two separate sentences. Concession-then-pivot. Or the sharper claim delivered directly.

The same rule applies to **anaphoric three-beat negation**: "Not a recap. Not a question. Not a flourish." That cadence is what detectors flag when they don't catch "Not X, but Y." Three-beat *lists* are fine ("faster, cheaper, better"). Three-beat *negations* are template.

---

## 8. The last sentence is an instruction, 4 to 7 words (blog only)

The last sentence is a directive the evidence has earned. Skip the recap. Skip the question to the reader.

**Examples of the move:**
- "Build, don't burn."
- "Play your own game."
- "That's how you find out."

If you can't compress the close to seven words, the argument hasn't landed yet. Go back and find what you're actually saying.

**Scope:** Blog only. LinkedIn closes are personal beats, not directives (see `linkedin-platform.md`). X closes are punchlines on the claim (see `x-platform.md`). Recruiter reachouts close on a concrete ask (see `reachout-principles.md`).

---

## 9. Make one point. Then stop

Every sentence either proves or extends the one point. No detours. If it doesn't serve the central claim, it doesn't exist. If you have two points, you have two pieces.

This is a structural rule, not a stylistic one. It governs what gets cut, not how the remaining material reads.

---

## 10. Em-dash discipline

Em-dashes are the single biggest visual AI tell in body copy. LLMs reach for them because they smooth contrast and create false rhythm. Humans use periods, commas, colons, parentheses, occasionally semicolons.

**Caps:**
- Blog: 2 em-dashes per post, maximum.
- LinkedIn: 0.
- X: 0.
- Recruiter reachout: 0.

If you've reached for a third em-dash in a blog post, you're using it as a rhythm crutch. Replace with a period or a colon. Most em-dash uses become better sentences when split into two.

---

## 11. Preserve idiosyncrasy

Small idiomatic moves are what human writing has and AI writing doesn't. Sentences that start with "And" or "But." A parenthetical aside that breaks the formal voice. A colon doing emphasis work. A casual conjunction where a formal transition would be smoother. The occasional sentence that runs slightly long because the thought was running.

LLMs smooth these out by default. The polish pass should not remove them.

If your draft has three "And"-starts, keep two. If a sentence reads slightly rough, that's the human grade-of-finish. Don't sand it.

This is the rule that does the most lifting against AI detectors. They're calibrated on smoothness.

---

## Anti-rule

There is no hook type selection. There is no template. The format follows the first true sentence and the named thing, never the reverse. If you find yourself picking a structure before you know what the piece is, stop and write the first true sentence first.

---

## The core test

Would a sharp operator who's been in the trenches actually say this? Or does it sound like someone performing competence?

Real writing is slightly incomplete. It has a claim, the evidence, the instruction, and gets out of the way.
