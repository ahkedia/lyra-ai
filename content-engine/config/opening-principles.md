# Opening Principles

Five principles for content openings. NOT templates — principles. Each with an example grounded in Akash's career.

---

## 1. Start with the true thing, not the smart-sounding thing

The first sentence is the argument, not a vehicle to reach it.

**Example:** "Most credit products die before the customer even applies. The failure isn't the product — it's the decision point 3 screens before."

---

## 2. Name what you see

When a recurring pattern emerges from experience, give it a term. Named phenomena spread.

**Example:** "The Correction Loop Gap" / "The Approval Rate Illusion" — not marketing terms, precision terms.

---

## 3. Scene or claim. Nothing in between

Open with a specific moment from the field, or open with a blunt claim. Never with premise-setting.

**Scene:** "We were 3 days from launch at CheQ when we pulled the approval threshold. Product thought it was a small call. It wasn't."

**Claim:** "Evaluation accuracy is the most misleading metric in AI products."

---

## 4. Make one point. Then stop

Every sentence either proves or extends the one point. No detours. If it doesn't serve the central claim, it doesn't exist.

---

## 5. Self-disclosure is authority

"We got this wrong at CheQ" is more credible than "most teams get this wrong." Specific failure, stated plainly, no apology.

---

## Anti-rule

There is no hook type selection. The format follows the first true sentence. Never the reverse.
