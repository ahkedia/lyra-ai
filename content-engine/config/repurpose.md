# Repurposing Chain

How a single idea travels across blog, LinkedIn, and X without feeling reformatted on either end.

## Sequencing (blog-first, default)

1. **Blog** is the seed. The essay does the full work: scene, pattern, named claim, evidence, implication, 4-7 word instruction (see voice-canon.md and MACRO_STRUCTURE.md).

2. **LinkedIn** takes one cut from the blog. It does not compress the whole essay into LinkedIn's space. Pick one of three formats from `linkedin-platform.md`. The argument-post format takes the named claim and 2-3 of the blog's evidence beats. The aphorism format takes the named claim alone. The scene format takes the blog's opening scene and ends on a personal coda instead of the blog's instruction.

3. **X** is the named claim, alone. One sentence or a thread of 2-4 short posts. No narrative scaffolding.

Blog-first matters because the named claim has to be earned before it travels. If you write the X post first and then try to back-fill the essay, the essay tends to read as a longer version of the same thought rather than the source it was meant to be.

## Three operational rules

### Rule 1: The named phrase is the through-line

The coined thing from voice-canon.md Rule 2 appears verbatim across all three surfaces. Same phrase, three contexts. This is what makes the X, LinkedIn, and blog versions feel connected without feeling redundant.

### Rule 2: Each surface gets one idea, in the format that surface allows

The blog runs the full arc. LinkedIn picks one of three formats based on whether the idea is best served as argument, aphorism, or scene. X compresses to the claim alone.

Do not map blog beats one-to-one to LinkedIn. The blog has its arc. LinkedIn has its own.

### Rule 3: Blog rules do not transfer to LinkedIn or X

The closing rule, the parallelism rule, the em-dash cap, and the macro structure all change at the surface boundary.

| Rule | Blog | LinkedIn | X |
|---|---|---|---|
| Close | 4-7 word instruction | Personal beat or named phrase | Punchline on the claim |
| Lists | Organic parallelism only | Numbered lists OK when shape is list-like | Line break between thoughts |
| Em-dashes | Max 2 per post | Zero | Zero |
| Concession | Avoid | "Yes X, AND Y" is native | Avoid |

## The litmus test (kept from prior version)

> If someone follows you on both X and LinkedIn, would seeing both feel redundant? If yes, you reformatted. If no, you rethought.

The operational rules above are how to pass this test, not just aspire to it.

## Newsletter (when enabled)

- Deep dive: 800-1,500 words
- Behind-the-scenes layer: the meeting, the decision, the number that the blog mentioned in passing
- Links to sources and related reading
- Subscriber-only data when relevant

The blog is public-facing argument. The newsletter is the working notebook the argument came from.
