# Macro Structure (blog only)

The default skeleton for blog posts. Not a template the writer fills in. A skeleton the writer breaks deliberately, not by accident.

If your draft doesn't follow this shape, the reason should be conscious. "I tried the skeleton and the piece wanted something else" is fine. "I never thought about it" means the piece is missing one of the beats.

## The skeleton

1. **Scene.** A specific moment from the field. Personal, time-stamped, named. 1-3 sentences. Or substitute a blunt claim if the abstraction is sharp enough to carry the opener (see Rule 3 in voice-canon.md).

2. **Pattern.** The thing you noticed across instances. The opening scene is one example. The pattern is what the example points to.

3. **Named claim.** The coined phrase from Rule 2 of the canon. The thing the title is named after. The phrase that will travel to LinkedIn and X.

4. **Evidence.** Two or three concrete cases with numbers and proper nouns. Companies. Dates. Dollar amounts. Specific decisions. The evidence section is where AI-generated drafts collapse, so it is the section that does the most work to prove the writing is yours.

5. **Implication.** What the evidence forces. The reader sees the pattern. The reader sees the cases. What is the reader now obligated to believe?

6. **Instruction.** 4-7 word close. The directive the implication earned (see Rule 8 of the canon).

## What the skeleton does not do

The skeleton is structural, not visual. A finished blog post has 2-5 visible sections titled by content, never by skeleton-beat.

Length stays governed by the cap in voice-canon.md and NEGATIVE_STYLE.md. The skeleton fits in 500 words or 1,100 words.

Beats 1 and 2 can swap. The named claim can land in paragraph 1 with a claim opener, or paragraph 3 with a scene opener. The instruction is always last.

## Scope

Blog only. LinkedIn picks one beat in the format that surface allows (see `linkedin-platform.md`). X compresses to the named claim alone (see `x-platform.md`). Recruiter reachouts use a different structure entirely (see `reachout-principles.md`).
